EMOTION GUIDE: SAD

PURPOSE
Use this guide when the detected emotional state is sad.
The aim is to reduce withdrawal, support gentle self-care, and encourage one small realistic action.

EVIDENCE BASIS
This guide is grounded in:
- WHO: Doing What Matters in Times of Stress (2020)
- NHS Every Mind Matters: Low mood guidance
- CDC emotional well-being: Sadness and depression guidance
- CBT background literature on behavioral activation and gentle coping

CORE INTERPRETATION
When the detected emotion is sadness, the response should meet the feeling with calm acknowledgement and low-pressure support.
The message should not push the user too hard, but it should still encourage a small step that can reduce withdrawal.

RESPONSE GOALS
- Validate the feeling softly without dwelling on it.
- Suggest one small manageable action.
- Support basic care such as rest, food, water, movement, daylight, or connection.
- Reduce isolation where appropriate.

RECOMMENDED SUPPORT DIRECTIONS
A good direction for sadness is to suggest one very small action that can gently restart momentum.
A good direction for sadness is to suggest a calming self-care action when energy feels low.
A good direction for sadness is to suggest light movement when that is practical.
A good direction for sadness is to suggest connection with a trusted person when that feels appropriate.
A good direction for sadness is to suggest daylight or fresh air when the context makes that realistic.

GOOD ACTION TYPES
A good action for sadness is to take a short walk when going outside is realistic and comfortable.
A good action for sadness is to stretch for a minute or two when a larger activity feels too heavy.
A good action for sadness is to drink some water or have a simple meal when basic care has been neglected.
A good action for sadness is to sit near daylight or step outside briefly when that is practical.
A good action for sadness is to do one familiar comforting activity that feels safe and manageable.
A good action for sadness is to message, call, or sit with a trusted person when connection feels possible.

STYLE RULES
- Use warm, steady, low-pressure wording.
- Do not sound excited.
- Do not minimize the feeling.
- Keep the action simple and immediate.

AVOID
- “Cheer up” style language.
- Overly positive reframing.
- Large tasks or productivity pressure.
- Language that rewards staying withdrawn.
- Intense self-improvement advice.

CONTEXT ADAPTATION HINTS
When it is night time, the best support for sadness leans toward rest, comfort, and winding down.
When it is morning or daytime, support for sadness can more often include gentle activation or daylight exposure.
When the weather is rainy, support for sadness should usually prefer indoor soothing actions.
When the user is at work, university, or another study setting, support for sadness should stay discreet and brief.
When the user is at home, support for sadness can more easily include comfort-based actions.
