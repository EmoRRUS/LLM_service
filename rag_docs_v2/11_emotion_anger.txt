EMOTION GUIDE: ANGER

PURPOSE
Use this guide when the detected emotional state is anger.
The aim is to lower physiological arousal and prevent impulsive reaction.

EVIDENCE BASIS
This guide is grounded in:
- WHO: Doing What Matters in Times of Stress (2020)
- CDC: Managing Difficult Emotions
- NHS: Get help with anger
- Gross (1998): emotion regulation framework

CORE INTERPRETATION
When the detected emotion is anger, the first priority is usually body-first regulation.
Immediate support should focus on slowing down, pausing, and reducing arousal before reflection or decision-making.

RESPONSE GOALS
- Create a pause.
- Lower physical tension.
- Avoid escalation.
- Suggest a safe reset action.

RECOMMENDED SUPPORT DIRECTIONS
A good direction for anger is to create a brief pause before the user speaks, replies, or acts.
A good direction for anger is to support slower breathing so physical arousal can come down.
A good direction for anger is to reduce muscle tension in the shoulders, jaw, or hands.
A good direction for anger is to suggest safe movement, such as walking or changing posture, when that is practical.
A good direction for anger is to postpone reaction until the body feels calmer.

GOOD ACTION TYPES
A good action to reduce anger is to take one slow breath and consciously relax the shoulders.
A good action to reduce anger is to step away for a minute when that is possible and safe.
A good action to reduce anger is to take a short walk or change posture so the body can reset.
A good action to reduce anger is to pause before replying, sending a message, or taking action.
A good action to reduce anger is to ground attention in the immediate environment until the intensity drops.

STYLE RULES
- Use steady, grounding language.
- Be calm and direct, but gentle.
- Keep the first action immediate and simple.

AVOID
- Encouraging confrontation.
- Validating lashing out.
- Highly analytical advice during peak anger.
- Moralizing language.
- Suggesting risky physical discharge.

CONTEXT ADAPTATION HINTS
When the user is at work, university, or another study setting, support for anger should usually suggest discreet regulation such as breathing or a short pause.
When the user is outside or commuting, support for anger should usually focus on grounding and safe movement.
When it is night time, support for anger should lean more strongly toward de-escalation and low stimulation.
When the weather is hot, support for anger can include cooling down and hydration when that fits the setting.
